Real Numbers — Chapter 1 (Worked Solutions & Explanations)

Key results, proofs and worked examples — neatly presented.

1. Euclid's Division Lemma

Statement: Given any two integers a and b (with b > 0), there exist unique integers q and r such that

a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b.

Proof (existence & uniqueness — sketch)

  1. Consider the set S = { a − bk | k ∈ ℤ and a − bk ≥ 0 } (non-empty for some k). Let r be the least element of S.
  2. By construction r ≥ 0 and r = a − bq for some integer q. If r ≥ b, then r − b = a − b(q+1) would be a smaller non-negative element of S, contradiction. So 0 ≤ r < b. This proves existence.
  3. Uniqueness: if a = bq1 + r1 = bq2 + r2 with 0 ≤ r1,r2 < b, subtract to get b(q1−q2) = r2−r1. The RHS has absolute value < b, so must be zero. Hence r1 = r2 and q1 = q2.

2. Euclid's Algorithm (Finding HCF)

Idea: Repeatedly apply Euclid's division lemma to compute the greatest common divisor (HCF) of two integers.

Worked Example 1 — HCF(330, 156)

330 = 156 × 2 + 18
156 = 18 × 8 + 12
18  = 12 × 1 + 6
12  = 6  × 2 + 0
→ HCF = 6

Worked Example 2 — HCF(435, 165)

435 = 165 × 2 + 105
165 = 105 × 1 + 60
105 = 60  × 1 + 45
60  = 45  × 1 + 15
45  = 15  × 3 + 0
→ HCF = 15

The HCF (greatest common divisor) is the last non-zero remainder.

Relation with LCM

For positive integers a and b: HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b. Example: for 330 and 156, HCF = 6 → LCM = (330×156)/6 = 8580.

3. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (FTA)

Statement: Every integer greater than 1 can be expressed as a product of primes, and this factorization is unique except for ordering.

Example

Take 504: prime factorization = 23 × 32 × 7.

Why uniqueness holds (outline)

Use proof by contradiction together with the property that if a prime divides a product then it divides at least one factor. This forces prime exponents to match in any two supposed distinct factorizations.

4. Irrational Numbers — Proofs

Example: √2 is irrational (classic proof)

  1. Assume √2 = p/q in lowest terms, p and q integers with gcd(p,q)=1.
  2. Square: 2 = p²/q² ⇒ p² = 2q². So p² is even ⇒ p is even ⇒ p = 2k.
  3. Then (2k)² = 2q² ⇒ 4k² = 2q² ⇒ q² = 2k² ⇒ q² even ⇒ q even.
  4. So both p and q are even → contradicts gcd(p,q)=1. Hence √2 is irrational.

√p is irrational for any prime p

Same style of proof works: assume √p = p1/q1 in lowest terms; then p divides p1², so p divides p1, write p1 = p·k and proceed to show q1 also divisible by p — contradiction.

General fact:

If n is not a perfect square, then √n is irrational. Proof uses prime factorization: in a perfect square all prime exponents are even; if n has a prime with odd exponent, √n would require fractional exponent — contradiction.

5. Decimal Expansions: Terminating & Repeating

Facts:

  • A rational number has either a terminating decimal expansion or a non-terminating repeating decimal expansion.
  • A rational number p/q in lowest terms terminates iff q's prime factors are only 2 and/or 5 (since decimal base is 10 = 2×5).
  • Irrational numbers have non-terminating, non-repeating decimals.

Examples

1/8 = 0.125 (terminating, because 8 = 2³). 1/6 = 0.1666... = 0.1(6) (repeating), 1/3 = 0.333... (repeating).

6. Typical Exercise Problems & Solutions

Problem A

Show that √3 is irrational.

Solution: Exactly like √2: assume √3 = p/q in lowest terms. Square and use parity reasoning or prime divisibility: p² = 3q² → 3 divides p² → 3 divides p → p = 3k → substitute and conclude 3 divides q — contradiction.

Problem B

Find HCF(504, 990) using Euclid's algorithm.

990 = 504 × 1 + 486
504 = 486 × 1 + 18
486 = 18 × 27 + 0
→ HCF = 18

Thus HCF(504,990) = 18 and LCM = (504 × 990)/18 = 27720.

Problem C

Use Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic to show that √12 is irrational or rational?

Solution: 12 = 22 × 3. Since in √12 the exponent of 3 is odd (1), √12 is not an integer. But √12 = 2√3. Because √3 is irrational, √12 is also irrational. (Note: √12 simplifies but not to a rational number.)

Problem D

Decide if 0.142857142857... is rational or irrational.

Solution: The block '142857' repeats → it's rational. In fact 0.(142857) = 1/7.

7. Summary / Quick Checklist

  • Euclid's Division Lemma gives quotient & remainder for integers.
  • Euclid's algorithm is the fastest method (by hand) for HCF.
  • FTA: unique prime factorization; useful for HCF/LCM and irrationality arguments.
  • Square roots of non-perfect squares are irrational.
  • Rational ↔ terminating or repeating decimals; irrational ↔ non-terminating non-repeating decimals.
