Class 10 Science Notes: Chapter 8 - Heredity

1. Introduction

Heredity is the biological process by which characteristics are transmitted from parents to their offspring. It ensures continuity of species and explains similarities between parents and children.

2. Mendel’s Experiments
  • Gregor Mendel studied inheritance patterns in pea plants (Pisum sativum).
  • Observed traits like seed shape, flower color, pod shape, etc.
  • Found that traits are passed from one generation to the next in a predictable manner.
3. Mendel’s Laws
  • Law of Segregation: Allele pairs separate during gamete formation, each gamete receives only one allele.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Alleles of different traits segregate independently during gamete formation.
4. Important Terms in Heredity
  • Gene: Unit of inheritance that controls a trait.
  • Allele: Different forms of the same gene.
  • Dominant allele: Expressed even if only one copy is present.
  • Recessive allele: Expressed only if both copies are present.
  • Genotype: Genetic makeup of an organism.
  • Phenotype: Observable traits of an organism.
  • Homozygous: Two identical alleles for a trait (AA or aa).
  • Heterozygous: Two different alleles for a trait (Aa).
5. Inheritance in Humans
  • Some traits are controlled by single genes (e.g., widow’s peak, blood group).
  • Sex determination: XY system (Male = XY, Female = XX).
  • Some disorders are inherited (e.g., color blindness, hemophilia).
6. Variation
  • Variations are differences in traits among individuals of the same species.
  • Types of variation:
    • Continuous variation: Traits show a range (e.g., height, weight).
    • Discontinuous variation: Traits have distinct categories (e.g., blood groups, tongue rolling).
  • Variation is caused by genetic factors (mutations, recombination) and environmental factors.
7. Quick Revision Tips
  • Remember Mendel’s experiments and laws clearly.
  • Know all key terms: gene, allele, genotype, phenotype, homozygous, heterozygous.
  • Understand examples of human traits controlled by single genes.
  • Differentiate continuous and discontinuous variation.
