Class 10 Science Notes: Chapter 6 - Control and Coordination

1. Introduction

Control and coordination are the life processes by which organisms regulate their activities and respond to internal and external stimuli to maintain homeostasis.

2. Nervous System in Animals
  • Consists of brain, spinal cord, and nerves.
  • Neurons are the functional units; they transmit electrical impulses.
  • Types of neurons:
    • Sensory neurons: carry impulses from receptors to CNS.
    • Motor neurons: carry impulses from CNS to effectors.
    • Interneurons: connect sensory and motor neurons in CNS.
  • Reflex action: rapid, automatic response to a stimulus (e.g., touching a hot object).
3. Structure of a Neuron
  • Cell body: contains nucleus and cytoplasm.
  • Dendrites: receive impulses from other neurons.
  • Axon: transmits impulses to other neurons or effectors.
  • Myelin sheath: insulating layer around axon that speeds up impulse transmission.
4. Brain and Spinal Cord
  • Brain is the control center of the body; it regulates voluntary and involuntary actions.
  • Parts of brain:
    • Cerebrum: thinking, memory, voluntary actions
    • Cerebellum: balance and coordination
    • Medulla oblongata: controls involuntary actions like heartbeat, breathing
  • Spinal cord: conducts impulses to and from the brain and controls reflex actions.
5. Human Eye
  • Sense organ for vision.
  • Parts:
    • Cornea: transparent layer, bends light
    • Iris: controls amount of light entering eye
    • Lens: focuses light on retina
    • Retina: contains photoreceptor cells (rods and cones)
    • Optic nerve: transmits impulses to brain
  • Defects of vision:
    • Myopia (nearsightedness)
    • Hypermetropia (farsightedness)
    • Cataract (clouding of lens)
6. Human Ear
  • Sense organ for hearing and balance.
  • Parts:
    • Outer ear: collects sound waves
    • Middle ear: contains ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) to transmit vibrations
    • Inner ear: cochlea for hearing; semicircular canals for balance
7. Plant Hormones
  • Plant growth and responses are controlled by hormones.
  • Types of plant hormones:
    • Auxins: promote cell elongation, phototropism, geotropism
    • Gibberellins: stimulate stem elongation, seed germination
    • Cytokinins: promote cell division, delay aging of leaves
    • Ethylene: promotes fruit ripening
    • Abscisic acid (ABA): induces dormancy, closes stomata
8. Tropic Movements in Plants
  • Growth movements in response to stimuli:
  • Phototropism: movement towards light
  • Geotropism/Gravitropism: movement in response to gravity
  • Hydrotropism: growth towards water
  • Thigmotropism: growth in response to touch (e.g., tendrils)
9. Quick Revision Tips
  • Remember the parts of the nervous system and their functions.
  • Know the structure of a neuron and how impulses are transmitted.
  • Learn the functions of human eye and ear along with common defects.
  • Understand plant hormones and their roles in tropic movements.
